# Drone Trajectory Evaluation Under Uncertainty Using Quantum Amplitude Estimation

## Introduction

Unmanned Traffic Management (UTM) is becoming increasingly important as unmanned aerial systems (UAS) are deployed in denser and more dynamic airspaces. In such settings, safe operation requires not only generating candidate trajectories, but also evaluating those trajectories quickly under uncertainty.

The main difficulty is that the quality of a trajectory depends on uncertain factors such as wind disturbances, temporary no-fly zones, dynamic obstacles, communication delays, and the trajectories of other drones. In practice, this turns trajectory evaluation into a probabilistic estimation problem rather than a purely deterministic shortest-path problem.

The purpose of this challenge is to investigate whether quantum computing can accelerate the evaluation of drone trajectories under uncertainty. The broader application context remains path planning, but the core technical focus of the assignment is trajectory evaluation using Quantum Amplitude Estimation (QAE).

## Quantum Perspective

Decomposing the problem of drone trajectory evaluation, the most relevant tasks are:

1. Identify how a candidate trajectory can be evaluated under uncertain environmental and operational conditions.
2. Study the classical probabilistic baseline, including Monte Carlo estimation of safety and quality metrics.
3. Investigate whether Quantum Amplitude Estimation can provide a meaningful asymptotic advantage for those estimation tasks.
4. Assess the practical feasibility of the solution, including oracle construction, circuit depth, qubit requirements, and the hardware assumptions needed for any potential advantage.

The key observation is that, once candidate paths are generated classically, their evaluation can be written as a probability or expectation under a distribution over uncertain scenarios. For a candidate path `p` and random scenario `omega ~ P`, one may define:

```text
a_p = E[q(p, omega)]
```

where `q(p, omega)` is a bounded path-quality score or a binary indicator such as:

1. collision-free completion,
2. arrival before a time threshold,
3. satisfaction of all safety constraints,
4. normalized travel-time or energy cost.

This makes trajectory evaluation a natural candidate for QAE.

## Classical Trajectory Evaluation

In a practical workflow, candidate paths may be generated by standard classical methods such as:

1. graph-based methods like Dijkstra or A*,
2. sampling-based methods such as RRT or RRT*,
3. trajectory optimization methods under deterministic constraints.

This assignment does not ask you to replace those generators with quantum methods. Instead, the classical baseline should focus on evaluating fixed candidate paths under uncertainty.

For each path `p`, the classical workflow is:

1. sample `N` scenarios `omega_1, ..., omega_N`,
2. simulate whether the path is safe or compute its bounded score in each scenario,
3. estimate the mean or probability of interest.

For example, for collision-free success one may define:

```text
I_safe(p, omega) = 1 if the path is collision-free in scenario omega, else 0
```

and estimate:

```text
hat(a_p) = (1/N) * sum_i I_safe(p, omega_i)
```

Likewise, one may estimate expected normalized cost:

```text
hat(c_p) = (1/N) * sum_i c(p, omega_i)
```

with `c(p, omega)` scaled to `[0,1]`.

To achieve additive error `epsilon`, standard Monte Carlo estimation requires `O(1/epsilon^2)` samples.

## Quantum Alternative: QAE

Quantum Amplitude Estimation is a natural quantum counterpart to Monte Carlo estimation. The target quantity for a fixed candidate path is either:

```text
a_p = Pr[path p is safe under omega]
```

or:

```text
a_p = E[q(p, omega)]
```

for a bounded path-quality score `q`.

At a high level, the quantum workflow should:

1. prepare a quantum state representing the uncertainty distribution over scenarios,
2. reversibly evaluate the safety or quality of a fixed path in each scenario,
3. encode the target quantity into a quantum amplitude,
4. use QAE to estimate that amplitude.

Under ideal oracle assumptions, QAE can reduce the query complexity from `O(1/epsilon^2)` to `O(1/epsilon)` for additive error `epsilon`. This is the main theoretical source of quantum advantage in this assignment.

The key caveat is that the speedup applies to the estimation layer only. Candidate-path generation remains classical, and the practical value of QAE depends heavily on the cost of state preparation and reversible path-evaluation logic.

## Suggested Project Scope

For this use case, we propose the following workflow:

1. Define a simplified airspace model, for example a small 2D or 3D grid with static and dynamic constraints.
2. Generate one or more candidate paths classically.
3. Define a probabilistic uncertainty model over environmental and operational disturbances.
4. Choose a target quantity such as collision-free probability, probability of on-time arrival, or expected normalized cost.
5. Implement a classical Monte Carlo baseline for evaluating candidate paths.
6. Formulate the same evaluation task as an amplitude-estimation problem.
7. Implement a proof-of-concept quantum workflow, either as a toy circuit simulation, a pseudocode-level oracle construction, or a resource-estimation study.
8. Prepare a feasibility study for the proposed approach.

You may start from a simplified model with:

1. a small `X x X` grid or graph,
2. a finite set of uncertain obstacle or traffic scenarios,
3. a binary success metric or a bounded path-quality score,
4. a small instance that is easy to explain and simulate.

## Solution Properties

The feasibility study should contain:

1. Executive summary, a clear one-pager describing the points below.
2. Mathematical problem definition.
3. Overview of classical tools used to generate and evaluate candidate trajectories, including theoretical computational complexity, performance estimate, and bottlenecks.
4. Overview of quantum algorithms suitable for the given problem, with emphasis on QAE, including a system-design exercise, computational complexity comparison to classical Monte Carlo, resource estimates, and a feasibility horizon in years.
5. Estimate of economic viability as a roadmap, with projected hardware expectations for the technology and the accuracy required.

The success of your proof-of-concept implementation will be evaluated based on achieving one or more of the following milestones:

1. Implement a trajectory-evaluation workflow centered on probabilistic path assessment under uncertainty.
2. Demonstrate consistency with a classical Monte Carlo baseline up to a tolerable approximation error.
3. Provide a correct mapping from a path-quality metric to the QAE formalism.
4. Compare the theoretical scaling of the classical and quantum approaches.
5. Provide a realistic assessment of the assumptions under which a quantum advantage could emerge.
